# Sword Worlds Culture Brain

## Purpose

This file is a writing tool, not a history summary. Use it to decide what a Sword Worlder notices, values, says, forgives, and refuses.

## Core Values

- Straightforward and worthy action
- Earned reputation over displayed credentials
- Hard work as civic contribution
- Preparedness as armor against fate
- Loyalty to family, world, and proven comrades
- Independence from outside control
- Social obligation to aid those who have contributed to society

## Reputation and Competence

Sword Worlders care intensely about how their actions will be judged. Respect comes from demonstrated skill, honesty, courage, and useful work. A title from a respected institution is provisionally credible, but poor conduct can strip it of value quickly.

Risk-taking is admired when the risk is understood and managed. Bold preparation earns respect. Recklessness caused by laziness, vanity, or ignorance does not.

A person who honestly admits that a task exceeds their skill may retain respect. Someone who pretends competence and fails others will not.

## Truth and Courtesy

Speaking an unwelcome truth is considered a hard courtesy. Enjoying another person's discomfort is unworthy, but hiding necessary truth behind polite lies can be an insult.

This creates friction with Imperial social customs, where ambiguity and face-saving may be considered tactful.

Sword Worlders are not incapable of deception. The distinction is relational:

- Friends and agreed contests deserve fair dealing.
- Promises and surrender terms should be honored.
- Enemies may be confused, trapped, and misdirected without shame.
- Clever deceit against a deserving opponent can be admired.

## Institutions and Authority

Sword Worlders do not automatically reject institutions. They judge institutions by how their members behave and whether the institution fulfills its obligations.

An institution that cannot protect, assist, or competently organize its people has failed its social contract. Ornamental authority earns little patience.

## Preparedness and Social Obligation

The idea that strength and preparation are armor against fate applies to disaster planning, equipment maintenance, household readiness, and the expectation that society will respond when citizens suffer misfortune.

A preventable disaster is evidence of incompetence. An unavoidable disaster followed by indifference is evidence of moral failure.

## Gender and Family

Strong traditional gender roles remain common. Girls are often guided toward lower-risk occupations, but women who choose hazardous or traditionally male professions generally expect to be treated as full members of that profession.

Within the role, a soldier is a soldier. Patronizing a proven professional is itself disreputable.

The deeper cultural concern is continuity of the community and the raising of children.

## Food and Aesthetics

Sword Worlders often favor the straightforward and worthy in food, tools, and design. A simple dish executed perfectly may receive more respect than elaborate presentation that obscures the quality of its ingredients.

They do not dislike sophistication. They dislike sophistication performed mainly to display status.

## Solveig Lens

Solveig should instinctively ask:

1. Who here can actually do the job?
2. Who has earned trust rather than requested it?
3. What is the worst plausible turn, and are we ready for it?
4. Is someone using procedure to avoid responsibility?
5. What truth is everyone being too polite to say?

She can respect competent Imperials while remaining suspicious of Imperial institutions. She need not be perpetually angry or theatrically Viking.

## Source Basis

Derived from *Sword Worlds* and *Behind the Claw*, especially material on reputation, hard courtesy, gender roles, preparedness, civic obligation, and acceptable deception.
